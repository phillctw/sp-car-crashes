A clear blue sky isn't always good news for drivers in São Paulo State, Brazil

If you think the danger on highways lies in bad weather, you might want to think again

By Phillippe Watanabe

If the sky is blue and the weather is calm, be aware, a reckless driver may be ahead.

On the highways of São Paulo, Brazil's most populous state, the weather apparently isn't to blame for car accidents. Data from DER/SP (São Paulo Roads Department) for 2025 so far shows that most of car crashes happen during normal weather conditions, when the drivers have good visibility.

So who is to blame?

Well, you might guess it: humans —or, more specifically, human recklessness.

Although there are many cases marked as "No information" or "Not informed", the data clearly shows that drivers' actions are the main cause of most accidents on São Paulo's roads.

The data also doesn't show any major differences in the types of accidents that occur when visibility is poor. The majority of accidents are rear-end collisions, which also account for the largest share of deaths on São Paulo's roads this year.

There is, however, a slight shift when it rains: in these conditions, crashes involving vehicles hitting guardrails top the list of accident types.

Of course accidents don't happen in a vacuum. It's reasonable to assume that, in general, areas with more vehicles and higher road activity are more likely to see accidents occur.

The concentration of accidents in some parts of São Paulo State also doesn't tell the whole story. After all, we care about accidents because they can cost people their lives. Brazil has some astonishing numbers when it comes to traffic-related deaths. In 2022 alone, 34,000 people died in traffic accidents in Brazil —a figure that has been growing in recent years. That year, about 16 out of every 100,000 Brazilians died in road accidents —a rate far above that of neighboring countries and most European nations.

But because Brazil is so big, there is no uniformity in traffic-related deaths. São Paulo, for example, is not among the states with the highest road fatality rates. Even so, the numbers for 2025 already look alarming: by May 26th, 472 people had lost their lives in traffic accidents in the state.

Time to rest (but without taking your eyes off the road)

So far this year on São Paulo's roads, anyone planning to hit the road on a Friday to relax somewhere else for the weekend should double their attention. Fridays see the highest number of accidents and traffic-related deaths. Weekend days also tend to have more severe accidents, accounting for a disproportionately high number of deaths compared to total crashes.

It's hard to find a silver lining when dealing with something as alarming as the number of accidents in Brazil and in São Paulo State. Maybe the only encouraging sign in the data is that far more people walk away unharmed (22,372) than lose their lives in highway crashes (472). But even so, that's no reason to be satisfied.
